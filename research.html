Research Summary

My masters thesis can be described as differentiating between stars that are "dead" and galaxies that are being "born". In other words, I am looking into new ways of discrimating between young supernovae (SNR) and faint active galactic nuclei (AGN) in dwarf galaxies. Identification and study of AGN in bulgeless/dwarf galaxies is extremely important inunderstanding the co-evolution of galaxies and super-massive blackholes. But due the faint emission of these low-mass AGN, they can often me confused with a young supernovae. As a high energy astrophysicist, my approach on this problem is to identify emission lines in the X-ray spectra and attribute the X-ray emission to high metallicity hot plasma in SNRs versus a simple absorbed power-law that describes most AGN emission.

As a Masters student who is still at the start of his career, I also like to explore other topics in astrophysics. In particular, I have also worked on understanding intra-binary shock and neutron star emission in eclipsing (redback) millisecond pulsar 47 Tuc W (PSR J0024-7204W), using the X-ray and optical light curves and the X-ray spectra. Currently, I am also attempting to study the nature of compact object in SNR 1E0102.2-7219 in SMC. To look into more details of my work during my undergraduate, please look into my CV.

Research Topics

  • Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN)

    Identify AGN in bulgeless galaxies

    AGN are centers of galaxies where the compact nuclear blackhole is rapidly accreting matter from the surrounding regions. These high energy objects harbour several physical processes, thus emitting radiations across teh entire electromagnetic spectrum. Recent discovery from IceCube show that AGN with ets pointed towards us could be the source of high energy neutrinos.

    I study the X-ray spectra to determine if the emission in from AGN or other sources like supernova remnants. We recently showed that the proposed candidate AGN in Henize 2-10 and NGC 4178, could more likely be a supernova remnant(Hebbar et al., in preparation)

    As a part of my masters thesis, I plan to develop an algorithm that can be used by X-ray CCD instruments to identify strongly-line dominated spectra even in low-count regime. Such a tool can allow us to recognize Fe-K lines in obscured AGN, Fe-L lines from chromospherically active stars, Si lines from young supernova remnants etc., thus allowing us to analyze the nature of the X-ray emission.

  • Supernova Remnant (SNR)

    Study X-ray emission of SNRs and compact objects in them

    SNRs are the aftermath of highly energetic supernova explosions. The hot plasma in the supernova results in the emission of electromagnetic(EM) radiation including X-rays. EM radiation can also be emitted due to the interactions of expanding material with the surrounding circum-stellar medium resulting in a forward shock.

    I enjoy performing spectral analysis on X-rays from SNRs for a better description of their structure. It has been argued that the broad lines in radio-quiet AGN could be produced by young SNRs in starburst galaxies. Thus a better understanding of their high metallicity line-dominated spectra could allow us to distinguish SNRS from AGN in low-count regime.

    I am also interested in understanding the nature of compact objects left behind in the SNRs. Investigating the X-ray spectra of compact object in SNR 1E0102.2-7219, has provided evidence towards its high magnetic field and also hinted towards the presence of a carbon atmosphere(Hebbar & Heinke, in preparation).

  • Millisecond Pulsars (MSPs)

    Investigate the X-ray variability and spectra of eclipsing MSPs

    Millisecond pulsars are a separate class of radio pulsars that have rotation periods of a few milliseconds. The small spin periods and low magnetic fields suggest towards a separate evolutionary channel for these pulsars. Detections of millisecond X-ray pulsations in accreting low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs), and the discovery of transient MSPs switching between the radio pulsar and LMXB phases have proven that MSPs are recycled neutron stars that are spun up due to accretion from a companion star.

    Studying the X-ray variability of eclipsing binary MSPs can help us understand the Intra-Binary shock caused by the interaction of pulsar wind and the wind from companion star. Constantly keeping track of this variability allows us to search for more transient MSPs. I have analyzed the X-ray emission from 47 Tuc W and argued for the variability in the source(Hebbar et al., in preparation). Along with the X-ray spectra and the optical light curves, we have attempted to model the IBS in the system.

  • Electromagnetic follow-up of gravitational wave detections

    Developed tools to detect EM counterpart and localize the source

    The detection of GW170817 triggered a new era of multi-messenger astronomy. The analysis of gravitational wave, and the subsequent electromagnetic follow-up confirmed the association between binary neutron star (BNS) merger and short gamma ray bursts (GRB) and provided evidence for the BNS mergers to be the origin of r-process elements

    In this era of transient astronomy it is important to have all-sky monitors that could watch the skies at all times. Due to the large field of view of these all sky monitors, localizing the source after detection could be confusing. I worked with Prof. Varun Bhalerao to develop a python package that could be used to localize the GRB and GW detections in such instruments on satellites orbiting the earth. Such a tool would be crucial for detectors like AstroSAT CZTI and future missions like LISA. In another project under Prof. Bhalerao, I looked for ways to automatize the search for GRBs in AstroSAT CZTI instrument.
